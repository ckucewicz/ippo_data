Skills alone won't get you hired.
The data job market is competitive. Two candidates can have identical technical skills, and the one who understands the business, the field, and the problems wins every time. That's why this site doesn't just teach you SQL and Python. It helps you think like a practitioner in your specific industry. A finance analyst and an environmental researcher might run the same regression, but they ask completely different questions, interpret results differently, and know which numbers actually matter. Domain knowledge is what makes you hireable.

Land the job.

Skills get you in the room. Strategy gets you the offer. Here's how to approach the job search intentionally.
The job search trifecta
Three things determine whether you land a data role. Most people only work on one of them.
1
Skills & Knowledge
Technical skills, domain knowledge, and a portfolio that proves you can do the work. This is what the roadmaps are for.
2
Networking
Most jobs are filled before they're posted. Building genuine relationships in your target field is how you get to interviews others never see.
3
Application Strategy
Applying to 100 jobs cold rarely works. A targeted, tailored approach to a handful of roles you actually want is far more effective.
Most people focus entirely on skills and then mass-apply to everything. That's the hardest path. The people who land roles fastest usually have all three working together, even modestly. You don't need to be an expert networker or write perfect cover letters. You just need to be intentional.
The targeted application approach
Instead of cold-applying to dozens of jobs and hearing nothing, build a short list of companies you genuinely want to work for and go deep on each one. This takes more effort per application, and gets dramatically better results.
1
Build your target list
Write down 10–20 companies you'd actually be excited to work at. Consider: industry, company size, mission, the kind of data problems they work on. Don't apply to any of them yet.
2
Research each company
Understand what they do, what their data team looks like, what problems they're trying to solve. Look at their blog, press releases, LinkedIn, job postings. This research will come up in interviews.
3
Find a way in
Before you apply, look for someone at the company on LinkedIn, ideally someone in the data team or a recruiter. Send a short, genuine message: who you are, why you're interested in their work specifically, and a question. Not asking for a job, asking to learn. A warm application is exponentially more effective than a cold one.
4
Tailor your application
When you do apply, customize your resume and any cover letter for that specific role. Mirror their language. Highlight the experience most relevant to their team. This is only possible because you're applying to a handful of roles, not a hundred.
5
Follow up and stay visible
If you don't hear back in 1–2 weeks, a short follow-up is appropriate. Continue engaging with their content on LinkedIn. Stay on their radar. Hiring happens in cycles, being remembered when the timing is right matters.

This approach is adapted from The 2-Hour Job Search by Steve Dalton, highly recommended reading.

Resume
Keep it focused
One page until you have 5+ years of experience. Lead with a 2-sentence summary: who you are, what role you're targeting, and your strongest credential. Every bullet should describe impact, not just activity.
Use numbers wherever you can
"Reduced query runtime by 40%" beats "improved query performance." Even rough estimates are better than none. Numbers make claims real.
Treat your projects like work experience
If you don't have a data job yet, your portfolio projects are your experience section. Include the problem you solved, what you built, and a GitHub or live link.

LinkedIn
Optimize your headline
Don't just write your current title. Write what you do and where you're heading. Example: "Aspiring Data Analyst · SQL · Python · Tableau · Open to Opportunities."
Post about your learning
Share project updates, things you learned, tools you explored. It doesn't need to be polished. Authenticity travels further than polish on LinkedIn, and consistency compounds over time.
Aim for 500+ connections
Connect with people in roles you want, recruiters at target companies, and peers learning alongside you. A short personal note with a connection request has a much higher acceptance rate than a blank one.
Use the Featured section
Pin your GitHub portfolio, a live Tableau dashboard, or a write-up of your best project. It's the first thing a recruiter looks at after your headline.

About
Why this site exists.
I didn't come from a traditional data or tech background. My path started in a math classroom, teaching high school students, trying to make abstract concepts feel real and useful. It was there that I fell in love with what data could do: not just numbers on a spreadsheet, but a lens for understanding people, systems, and problems. Without a formal tech degree, I know firsthand how overwhelming it can feel to try to break into this field, and how hard it is to find guidance that actually speaks to where you're starting from.
When I decided to make the transition into data, I quickly realized how overwhelming the landscape is. There are hundreds of courses, bootcamps, YouTube channels, and blog posts, all telling you different things about where to start, what to learn, and what employers actually want. Most of the resources I found were generic. They didn't account for the fact that a researcher in public health thinks about data completely differently than an analyst at a tech startup. They didn't tell you that domain knowledge often matters as much as technical skills. And they definitely didn't help you figure out how to break in without a computer science degree.
So I built the guide I wish I'd had.
About the name
Ippo (一歩) is Japanese for "one step." The name comes from the Lao Tzu proverb — a journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step. Every data career starts somewhere. This is where yours does.
This site is for people who are earlier in that journey, whether you're a teacher, a policy analyst, a recent graduate, or someone who just realized they want to work with data. It's not a course. It's not trying to sell you anything. It's a roadmap, built by someone who has been through it, designed to give you orientation and direction without the noise.
The site is still growing. If something is wrong, missing, or could be better, please tell me. That feedback is how this gets more useful for the next person who lands here.
